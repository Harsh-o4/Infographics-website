Twitter launched in 2006 with a simple idea: short messages. Users could post “tweets” up to 140 characters at first. It was fast and different. People loved it.


Twitter became a place for news and quick thoughts. Celebrities, politicians, and regular people used it. It grew into a global conversation tool.


Twitter brought a new style to social media. Unlike Facebook’s long posts, tweets were short and snappy. At first, the limit was 140 characters, later doubled to 280. People used it to share ideas, jokes, or breaking news. During big events—like elections or sports—Twitter buzzed with activity. Hashtags, like #ThrowbackThursday, made it easy to find topics. By 2010, it had millions of users tweeting every day. It wasn’t about fancy pages; it was about speed and simplicity. Famous people like Barack Obama or Taylor Swift joined, making it even bigger. Twitter showed that less could be more. It’s still a key player today.


Twitter also changed how we get news. Reporters and eyewitnesses post updates live. It’s like a megaphone for the world.
